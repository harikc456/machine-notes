# Grokking: Generalization Beyond Overfitting on Small Algorithmic Datasets

**Authors:** Alethea Power, Yuri Burda, Harri Edwards, Igor Babuschkin, Vedant Misra  
**Affiliation:** OpenAI / Google  
**Venue:** arXiv preprint, January 2022  
**arXiv:** 2201.02177

## Summary

Documents a striking training phenomenon on small algorithmic datasets: neural networks first rapidly memorize the training set (near-zero training loss, near-chance validation accuracy), then after much more training, abruptly generalize (validation accuracy jumps to near-100%). The authors call this "grokking."

## Setup

- Tasks: modular arithmetic (a + b mod p, a · b mod p), permutation group compositions, sparse binary operations
- Models: small transformers (2-layer, varying width); some MLP experiments
- Datasets: 100–10,000 examples; all inputs enumerable (finite groups)
- Training: AdamW with weight decay; 90%/10% train/val splits typical

## Key Findings

1. **Delayed generalization:** Validation accuracy improves orders of magnitude more slowly than training accuracy; grokking often occurs tens of thousands of steps after memorization.
2. **Weight decay is critical:** Without weight decay, models memorize but never grok. With sufficient weight decay, generalization eventually occurs.
3. **Data fraction matters:** With more training data (higher fraction of all pairs), grokking happens faster.
4. **The regime is real:** Multiple tasks and architectures exhibit it; not a training artifact.

## Weight Norm Dynamics (key mechanistic insight)

During grokking, the weight norms first increase (memorization phase) then decrease (generalization phase). This norm reduction corresponds to learning a more compact, generalizable circuit. See [[clip-to-grok]] for a follow-on showing that clipping weight norms directly accelerates grokking 39–249×.
